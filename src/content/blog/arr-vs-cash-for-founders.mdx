## Does ARR Equal Cash Flow?

<p>No. <strong>ARR does not equal cash flow.</strong></p>

<p>ARR is a revenue metric. Cash flow is a liquidity metric.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">ARR tells you:</p>

<blockquote className="my-6 border-l-4 border-primary/30 pl-4 not-italic text-muted-foreground">
  <p>How much recurring revenue your business would generate annually based on current subscriptions.</p>
</blockquote>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Cash flow tells you:</p>

<blockquote className="my-6 border-l-4 border-primary/30 pl-4 not-italic text-muted-foreground">
  <p>How much money actually moved into or out of your bank account.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The distinction matters because companies spend cash, not ARR.</p>

<p>Payroll is paid with cash. Vendors are paid with cash. Taxes are paid with cash. Runway is measured in cash.</p>

<p>This is why founders who focus exclusively on ARR often find themselves surprised by cash shortages. For the broader forecasting framework, see our guide on <a href="/blog/what-is-cash-flow-forecasting" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">cash flow forecasting</a>.</p>

## What Is the Difference Between ARR and Cash?

<p>A useful way to think about <strong>ARR vs cash</strong>:</p>

### ARR Measures Economic Value

<p>ARR answers:</p>

<blockquote className="my-6 border-l-4 border-primary/30 pl-4 not-italic text-muted-foreground">
  <p>How much recurring revenue have we sold?</p>
</blockquote>

### Cash Measures Timing

<p>Cash answers:</p>

<blockquote className="my-6 border-l-4 border-primary/30 pl-4 not-italic text-muted-foreground">
  <p>When did the customer actually pay us?</p>
</blockquote>

<p>These questions are related. But they are not the same. The difference becomes obvious when we look at a real contract.</p>

## A $120,000 Annual Contract Walkthrough

<p>Imagine you close a customer on January 1st.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Contract value:</p>

<p><strong>$120,000 per year</strong></p>

<p>The customer prepays the entire amount upfront.</p>

### What Happens to Cash?

<p>On January 1st:</p>

<p><strong>Bank balance +$120,000</strong></p>

<p>The money immediately appears in your account. Cash flow improves instantly.</p>

### What Happens to ARR?

<p>ARR increases by <strong>$120,000</strong> also immediately.</p>

### What Happens to Revenue?

<p>This is where <strong>revenue recognition vs cash</strong> diverges. Accounting rules generally require revenue to be recognized over the service period.</p>

<p>That means <strong>$10,000 revenue per month</strong> for the next 12 months.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Month-by-month:</p>

| Month | Cash Received | Revenue Recognized |

| February | $0 | $10,000 |
| March | $0 | $10,000 |
| April | $0 | $10,000 |
| May | $0 | $10,000 |
| June | $0 | $10,000 |
| July | $0 | $10,000 |
| August | $0 | $10,000 |
| September | $0 | $10,000 |
| October | $0 | $10,000 |

## What Is Deferred Revenue?

<BlogFigure
  src="/blog/arr-vs-cash-for-founders/from-contract-to-cash.png"
  alt="From contract to cash flowchart showing contract signed, customer pays, deferred revenue liability, revenue recognized over time on the P&L, and cash remaining in the bank"
  caption="Cash hits the bank when the customer pays. Revenue is recognized over time. Deferred revenue bridges the gap on the balance sheet until service is delivered."

<p>The missing piece is deferred revenue.</p>

<p>When the customer prepays, cash increases immediately. But the company has not yet delivered 12 months of service.</p>

<p>The portion not yet earned becomes <strong>deferred revenue</strong>.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Deferred revenue represents:</p>

<blockquote className="my-6 border-l-4 border-primary/30 pl-4 not-italic text-muted-foreground">
  <p>Cash collected today for services that will be delivered later.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>This is one reason SaaS founders often see healthy bank balances even when recognized revenue appears modest. The reverse can also happen: revenue may look healthy while cash remains constrained.</p>

<p>Understanding <strong>deferred revenue cash flow</strong> and <strong>deferred revenue examples SaaS</strong> is critical for forecasting cash flow accurately. Accounting systems like <a href="/integrations/quickbooks" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">QuickBooks</a> track deferred revenue on the balance sheet; your forecast should still model when cash actually hits the bank.</p>

## Why Is My ARR Growing but Cash Flat?

<p>This is one of the most common questions founders ask finance teams: <strong>why is my ARR growing but cash not increasing</strong>?</p>

<p>Several factors can cause it.</p>

### Monthly Billing vs Annual Billing

<p>Imagine two companies. Both have <strong>$1M ARR</strong>.</p>

<p>Company A bills annually. Company B bills monthly. Their ARR is identical. Their cash flow is not.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Company A (annual billing):</p>

<ul>
  <li>customer pays $12,000 upfront</li>
  <li>cash enters immediately</li>
  <li>runway improves instantly</li>
  <li>deferred revenue increases</li>
  <li>bank balance grows</li>
</ul>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Company B (monthly billing):</p>

<ul>
  <li>customer pays $1,000 per month</li>
  <li>cash arrives gradually across twelve payments</li>
  <li>the company receives the same amount over a year</li>
  <li>ARR remains identical while cash timing changes dramatically</li>
</ul>

<p>This is the core of <strong>annual billing vs monthly billing cash flow</strong> and <strong>recurring revenue vs cash</strong>. For a rolling weekly view of when collections land, see our guide on <a href="/blog/what-is-a-13-week-cash-flow-forecast" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">13-week cash flow forecasting</a>.</p>

## Why Can Two Companies With the Same ARR Have Different Runway?

<p>Let's compare two SaaS businesses. Both generate <strong>$1M ARR</strong>. Both have identical margins and identical expenses. The only difference is billing structure.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Company A (annual prepaid):</p>

<ul>
  <li>cash collected upfront: $1,000,000</li>
  <li>immediate liquidity and longer runway</li>
  <li>stronger balance sheet and greater flexibility</li>
</ul>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Company B (monthly billing):</p>

<ul>
  <li>monthly collections: roughly $83,333 per month</li>
  <li>same ARR, very different cash position</li>
</ul>

<p>If revenue growth slows unexpectedly, Company B may encounter runway pressure far sooner. This is why investors often care about billing mix in addition to ARR.</p>

## Is Annual Billing Better Than Monthly Billing?

<p>For cash flow, often yes. For customers, not always.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Annual contracts and <strong>annual prepaid contracts</strong> create:</p>

<ul>
  <li>better <strong>annual billing cash flow</strong></li>
  <li>more predictable collections</li>
  <li>longer runway</li>
  <li>lower churn risk</li>
  <li>a stronger deferred revenue position</li>
</ul>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Monthly billing creates:</p>

<ul>
  <li>lower upfront commitment</li>
  <li>easier customer acquisition</li>
  <li>greater flexibility for buyers</li>
</ul>

<p>There is no universal answer. But founders should understand the tradeoff. Billing structure affects cash flow more than ARR.</p>

## How Much Runway Can Annual Billing Add?

<p>Consider a company spending <strong>$100,000 per month</strong> with net burn of <strong>$100,000 per month</strong>.</p>

<p>Now imagine the company converts several large customers from monthly billing to annual prepaid contracts.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">The result:</p>

<ul>
  <li>immediate cash infusion</li>
  <li>lower short-term liquidity risk</li>
  <li>more months of runway</li>
</ul>

<p>The ARR may not change at all. The bank balance changes dramatically. This is <strong>how annual contracts affect cash flow</strong> and why <strong>annual prepaid contracts and runway</strong> are linked strategically.</p>

<p>Annual prepayments are often used by SaaS companies preparing for uncertain fundraising environments. ## Why Revenue Recognition and Cash Collection Are Different

<p>Founders frequently ask: <strong>why revenue does not match bank balance</strong>?</p>

<p>Because <strong>revenue recognition SaaS</strong> follows accounting rules. Cash collection follows customer payment behavior.</p>

<p>Revenue can be earned before cash is collected. Cash can be collected before revenue is earned. The two timelines rarely align perfectly.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">This is why finance teams track simultaneously:</p>

<ul>
  <li>ARR</li>
  <li>revenue</li>
  <li>deferred revenue</li>
  <li>cash flow</li>
  <li>bank balances</li>
</ul>

<p>Each metric answers a different question.</p>
