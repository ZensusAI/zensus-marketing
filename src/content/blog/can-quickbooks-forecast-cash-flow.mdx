## What Is QuickBooks Cash Flow Planner?

<p><strong>QuickBooks Cash Flow Planner is a built-in forecasting tool that estimates future cash inflows and outflows using data already stored inside QuickBooks.</strong></p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">The planner uses:</p>

<ul>
  <li>connected bank account activity</li>
  <li>unpaid invoices</li>
  <li>unpaid bills</li>
  <li>future due dates stored in QuickBooks</li>
  <li>predictions generated by QuickBooks</li>
  <li>manual planning entries added by users</li>
</ul>

<p>According to Intuit, the planner is designed to help businesses understand expected cash movement over the next one to three months.</p>

<p>For many owner-operated businesses, this provides a useful short-term view of cash.</p>

## Does QuickBooks Do Cash Flow Forecasting?

<p>Yes. But it is important to understand the type of forecasting QuickBooks provides.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Quick answer:</p>

<p><strong>QuickBooks forecasts expected cash movement over the next 30 to 90 days using existing accounting and banking data.</strong></p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">It is primarily designed for:</p>

<ul>
  <li>short-term planning</li>
  <li>managing upcoming bills</li>
  <li>tracking expected collections</li>
  <li>understanding near-term cash availability</li>
</ul>

<p>It is not designed to function as a complete startup financial planning platform.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Most founders asking about:</p>

<ul>
  <li>runway forecasting</li>
  <li>fundraising planning</li>
  <li>hiring scenario modeling</li>
  <li>board reporting</li>
  <li>annual cash projections</li>
</ul>

<p>typically need forecasting beyond the Cash Flow Planner's intended scope. For the broader framework, see our guide on <a href="/blog/what-is-cash-flow-forecasting" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">cash flow forecasting</a>.</p>

## What Is the Forecasting Horizon in QuickBooks?

<p><strong>QuickBooks Cash Flow Planner is primarily built around a 30- to 90-day forecasting window.</strong></p>

### Definition

<p><strong>Forecast horizon</strong> refers to how far into the future a forecast projects.</p>

<p>QuickBooks explicitly describes Cash Flow Planner as helping users understand cash flow over the next one to three months.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">For many businesses, this is practical because:</p>

<ul>
  <li>payroll occurs monthly</li>
  <li>bills are paid within weeks</li>
  <li>collections happen relatively quickly</li>
</ul>

<p>However, startup planning often requires much longer visibility. Founders frequently need answers to questions such as:</p>

<ul>
  <li>how long is our runway?</li>
  <li>when do we need to raise?</li>
  <li>can we afford three new hires?</li>
  <li>what happens if revenue grows 20% slower?</li>
</ul>

<p>Those decisions often require six, twelve, or even eighteen months of forecasting. A <a href="/blog/what-is-a-13-week-cash-flow-forecast" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">13-week cash flow forecast</a> is the standard next step for weekly liquidity reviews; many boards expect even longer rolling views.</p>

## What Data Does QuickBooks Use for Future Cash Flow?

<p>QuickBooks relies heavily on transactions and due dates already present in your accounting system.</p>

<p><strong>A cash flow forecast is only as accurate as the assumptions and inputs behind it.</strong></p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">QuickBooks uses:</p>

<ul>
  <li>open invoices</li>
  <li>open bills</li>
  <li>connected banking data</li>
  <li>scheduled obligations</li>
  <li>planner entries</li>
</ul>

<p>This approach is convenient because setup is minimal. However, it also creates limitations when future cash events are not yet represented inside QuickBooks.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Examples include:</p>

<ul>
  <li>planned fundraising rounds</li>
  <li>future hiring plans</li>
  <li>future software contracts</li>
  <li>revenue growth assumptions</li>
  <li>sales pipeline forecasts</li>
</ul>

<p>If the data is not in QuickBooks, the planner generally cannot forecast it automatically.</p>

## Does QuickBooks Count Unpaid Invoices in Cash Flow Forecasts?

<p>Yes. Intuit states that future cash flow calculations can include unpaid invoices that are due in the future.</p>

### Why This Matters

<p>An invoice is not cash. An invoice is a request for payment.</p>

<p>Consider this example.</p>

### Example: $120,000 Annual Contract

<p>A startup signs a customer on January 1.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Contract details:</p>

<ul>
  <li>contract value: $120,000 annually</li>
  <li>invoice issued: $120,000</li>
  <li>payment terms: Net 60</li>
</ul>

<p>The forecast may assume collection based on the invoice due date. But reality may look different. For why ARR and bank balance diverge on prepaid contracts, see <a href="/blog/arr-vs-cash-for-founders" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">ARR vs cash for founders</a>.</p>

| Month | Forecast Assumption | Actual Cash |
| --- | --- | --- |
| January | Expected payment | $0 |
| February | Expected payment | $0 |
| March | Customer pays late | $120,000 |

<BlogFigure
  src="/blog/can-quickbooks-forecast-cash-flow/revenue-vs-cash-timeline.png"

<p>If collections slip, forecast accuracy decreases. This is not unique to QuickBooks. Every forecasting system must make assumptions. The important point is understanding what assumptions are being made.</p>

## Can QuickBooks Handle Multi-Currency Cash Flow Forecasting?

<p>Not through Cash Flow Planner.</p>

<p>According to Intuit documentation:</p>

<blockquote className="my-6 border-l-4 border-primary/30 pl-4 not-italic text-muted-foreground">
  <p>The cash flow planner isn't available if Multicurrency is on.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Intuit also states that multicurrency transactions are excluded from cash flow calculations.</p>

### Why This Matters

<p>Many startups today operate internationally.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Examples include:</p>

<ul>
  <li>US customers paying in USD</li>
  <li>European customers paying in EUR</li>
  <li>contractors paid in INR</li>
  <li>vendors paid in GBP</li>
</ul>

<p>Once multiple currencies enter the picture, cash forecasting becomes more complex. You need visibility into exchange rate exposure, currency-specific balances, and cross-border cash movement. Cash Flow Planner is not designed for that use case.</p>

## What Does QuickBooks Cash Flow Planner Not Include?

<p>According to Intuit documentation, cash flow calculations do not include certain transaction types.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Examples include:</p>

<ul>
  <li>multicurrency transactions</li>
  <li>journal entries</li>
  <li>some manually entered transactions</li>
  <li>certain disconnected account activity</li>
</ul>

### Why Founders Should Care

<p>Founders often manage important cash events outside day-to-day bookkeeping.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Examples:</p>

<ul>
  <li>venture debt</li>
  <li>SAFE financing</li>
  <li>equity raises</li>
  <li>future hiring plans</li>
  <li>planned office expansion</li>
  <li>strategic acquisitions</li>
</ul>

<p>These decisions may not exist as accounting transactions yet. As a result, they are difficult to incorporate into operational forecasts using accounting data alone.</p>

## When Is QuickBooks Cash Flow Planner Enough?

<p>For many businesses, Cash Flow Planner is genuinely useful.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">It works particularly well when:</p>

<ul>
  <li>operations are relatively stable</li>
  <li>forecasts are short term</li>
  <li>revenue is predictable</li>
  <li>collections are consistent</li>
  <li>single-currency accounting is used</li>
</ul>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Typical good fit:</p>

<ul>
  <li>local service businesses</li>
  <li>agencies</li>
  <li>professional firms</li>
  <li>small ecommerce businesses</li>
  <li>early-stage companies managing monthly operations</li>
</ul>

<p>For these businesses, a 90-day view often covers the most important decisions.</p>

## When Do Startups Outgrow QuickBooks Cash Flow Forecasting?

<p>Most startups outgrow Cash Flow Planner when they begin making decisions based on future scenarios instead of historical accounting data.</p>

<BlogFigure
  src="/blog/can-quickbooks-forecast-cash-flow/short-term-planning-to-strategic-forecasting.png"
  alt="Short-term cash planning versus strategic forecasting comparison: QuickBooks covers 30 to 90 days, collections, bills, and cash position while dedicated tools add runway, hiring, fundraising, and what-if scenarios"
  caption="Cash Flow Planner covers short-term liquidity. Strategic forecasting adds runway, hiring, fundraising, and scenario planning."
/>

### Common Signals

#### You Need Runway Forecasting

<p><strong>Runway is calculated by dividing available cash by monthly net burn.</strong></p>

<p>Formula: <strong>Runway = Cash on Hand ÷ Monthly Net Burn</strong></p>

<p>Founders rarely need only a 90-day view. They need to know cash position six months from now, twelve months from now, and fundraising deadlines.</p>

<p>A dedicated <a href="/tools/runway-calculator" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">runway calculator</a> becomes more useful at this stage. For burn vocabulary and 2026 benchmarks, see <a href="/blog/runway-vs-burn-rate-for-founders" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">runway vs burn rate</a>.</p>

#### You Need Scenario Planning

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Examples:</p>

<ul>
  <li>what if revenue grows 30% slower?</li>
  <li>what if hiring happens two months earlier?</li>
  <li>what if fundraising closes late?</li>
</ul>

<p>These are strategic planning questions rather than bookkeeping questions.</p>

#### You Need Board-Level Forecasting

<p>Investors often expect monthly forecast updates, burn analysis, runway tracking, and scenario models. Accounting forecasts and board forecasts are not always the same thing.</p>

## Accounting vs Forecasting

<p><strong>Accounting records what happened. Forecasting estimates what may happen in the future.</strong></p>

<p>QuickBooks Cash Flow Planner sits between the two: it projects near-term cash using data already in your books. That is valuable for operational liquidity. It is not the same as a startup planning model that layers hiring, fundraising, pipeline, and scenario assumptions on top of accounting truth.</p>

<p>The most effective founders do not choose between accounting and forecasting. They use both.</p>

## Final Thoughts

<p>QuickBooks can absolutely help forecast cash flow.</p>

<p>For many businesses, Cash Flow Planner provides a useful view of expected cash movement over the next 30 to 90 days. It uses existing accounting data, connected banking activity, unpaid invoices, unpaid bills, and future-dated obligations to estimate where cash is heading.</p>

<p>The key is understanding what problem it was built to solve.</p>

<p>If your goal is managing near-term liquidity, paying bills on time, and understanding expected collections, Cash Flow Planner can be extremely helpful.</p>

<p>If your goal is managing startup runway, fundraising timelines, hiring plans, board reporting, or multiple future scenarios, you may eventually need forecasting tools that extend beyond accounting data.</p>

<p>Accounting explains where cash has been. Forecasting helps determine where it is going next.</p>
