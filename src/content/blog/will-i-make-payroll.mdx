  To know whether you'll make payroll, project your available cash forward to the payroll date, subtract every committed outflow due before then, add only the inflows you're confident will clear in time, and compare the result to your minimum cash buffer. If the projected balance stays above your buffer, you'll make payroll. If it dips below, you have a gap, and the whole point is to see it weeks ahead, not the morning the run is due.
</p>

<p>Most founders answer "will I make payroll?" by glancing at their bank balance and doing some quick mental math. That feels reassuring, but the balance you see today is not the balance you'll have on payroll day. Between now and then, an annual contract might land, a big customer might pay three weeks late, rent and vendor bills clear, a tax payment goes out, and payroll itself debits your account. Whether you can cover it comes down to the <em>timing</em> of all those flows, and a single balance can't show you that.</p>

<p>This guide walks through a simple, repeatable method to answer the question with confidence, a worked example you can copy, and a checklist you can run before every payroll cycle. The method is <a href="/blog/what-is-cash-flow-forecasting" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">cash flow forecasting</a>, narrowed to the one date that matters most.</p>

## The method: project to the payroll date

<p>The reliable way to know if you'll make payroll is to build a short cash projection that ends on your payroll date. You don't need a finance degree or a 40-tab spreadsheet. You need five inputs.</p>

### 1. Start with your available cash, not your balance

<p>Begin with the cash you can actually use today: your bank balance minus any payments that have already been authorized but haven't cleared (pending card charges, scheduled transfers, checks you've sent). This is your true starting point.</p>

### 2. List every payroll date you're checking

<p>Write down the next one to three payroll runs and their exact dates and amounts (gross pay plus employer taxes and benefits, the full amount that leaves your account, not just net pay). Remember that employment tax deposits follow the <a href="https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/depositing-and-reporting-employment-taxes" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">IRS deposit schedule</a>, monthly or semiweekly, so put those on their own dates too. Most shortfalls hide one cycle out, so always check at least two.</p>

### 3. Add the inflows that will realistically clear in time

<p>List the money you expect to receive <strong>before</strong> each payroll date, and be honest about timing, not hopeful:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Invoices due, dated by when the customer <em>actually</em> pays, not the due date (if a client always pays net-45, use net-45)</li>
  <li>Subscription or recurring revenue, on the day it hits your bank; annual and quarterly contracts land in lumps, not smooth monthly slices</li>
  <li>Any financing, refunds, or transfers you've confirmed</li>
</ul>

<p>Leave out anything you only <em>hope</em> closes. An optimistic forecast that misses is worse than no forecast.</p>

### 4. Subtract every committed outflow due before then

<p>Subtract all the cash leaving your account before each payroll date:</p>

<ul>
  <li>The payroll run itself (the full loaded cost)</li>
  <li>Rent, software, and recurring vendor payments</li>
  <li>Loan or debt repayments</li>
  <li>Estimated taxes and any one-off bills you've committed to</li>
</ul>

### 5. Compare each payroll date to your cash buffer

<p>For each payroll date, the projected balance is: starting cash, plus inflows that clear before the date, minus outflows due before the date. Compare that number to your <strong>minimum cash buffer</strong>, the floor you never want to drop below. If you stay above it, you'll make payroll comfortably. If you land below it, you've found a gap with enough lead time to do something about it.</p>

## A worked example

<p>Say it's June 1. Your numbers look like this (figures are illustrative):</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Available cash today:</strong> $80,000</li>
  <li><strong>Payroll:</strong> $42,000 fully loaded, run on the 15th and the 30th</li>
  <li><strong>Minimum cash buffer:</strong> $25,000</li>
</ul>

<p>Now lay the flows on a calendar:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>June 5</strong>, rent + software clear: <strong>−$12,000</strong></li>
  <li><strong>June 12</strong>, a customer pays an overdue invoice: <strong>+$30,000</strong></li>
  <li><strong>June 15</strong>, payroll run #1: <strong>−$42,000</strong></li>
  <li><strong>June 22</strong>, quarterly contract finally lands: <strong>+$18,000</strong></li>
  <li><strong>June 30</strong>, payroll run #2: <strong>−$42,000</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Walk the balance forward to each payroll date:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Before June 15 payroll:</strong> 80,000 − 12,000 + 30,000 = $98,000, then −42,000 = <strong>$56,000</strong>. Well above the $25,000 buffer. ✅ You make this one easily.</li>
  <li><strong>Before June 30 payroll:</strong> 56,000 + 18,000 = $74,000, then −42,000 = <strong>$32,000</strong>. Still above $25,000, but only by $7,000. ⚠️</li>
</ul>

<p>The bank balance on June 1 ($80,000) looked like nearly two payrolls of cushion. The projection tells the real story: the second run lands you within $7,000 of your floor, and that's <em>only</em> if the quarterly contract clears on the 22nd as expected. If it slips to July, June 30 payroll drops you to $14,000, below your buffer. You now know, on June 1, exactly which inflow to chase and by when.</p>

<p>That gap is invisible on a bank statement and invisible in a flat "monthly revenue minus monthly expenses" model. It only shows up when you place each flow on the date it actually moves.</p>

## The mistake most founders make

<p>The single most common payroll-cash error is using <strong>averages instead of timing</strong>. "We bring in about $120k a month and spend about $100k, so we're fine" hides the fact that the $120k arrives in two lumps on the 12th and 27th while payroll, rent, and a tax bill all clear before the 15th. Monthly averages smooth over exactly the timing mismatches that cause a missed payroll.</p>

<p>The second mistake is <strong>optimistic receivables</strong>: assuming customers pay on the due date. Date your inflows by when each customer actually pays, and your forecast stops lying to you.</p>

## How far ahead should you forecast payroll?

<p>At least two full payroll cycles, roughly 30 to 60 days for most businesses. One cycle isn't enough: if you only look at the next run, you can clear it and still be walking into a wall on the one after. Two cycles gives you the lead time to collect a receivable, delay a discretionary payment, draw on a line of credit, or trim spend, all of which take days or weeks, not hours. If you want a longer horizon, a <a href="/blog/what-is-a-13-week-cash-flow-forecast" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">13-week cash flow forecast</a> extends the same discipline a full quarter out.</p>

## What's a safe cash buffer before payroll?

<p>Keep at least one full payroll run in reserve above your projected low point, and ideally two. As a broader floor, many founders target two to six months of total operating expenses. The lumpier and less predictable your revenue, the larger that buffer should be: a business living on a few large annual contracts needs more cushion than one with smooth monthly subscriptions, because a single late payment moves the needle further. For context on how thin most buffers run: JPMorgan Chase Institute's analysis of nearly 600,000 small businesses found the <a href="https://www.jpmorganchase.com/institute/all-topics/business-growth-and-entrepreneurship/report-cash-flows-balances-and-buffer-days" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">median holds just 27 cash buffer days</a>, under a month of typical outflows.</p>

## Your "will I make payroll" checklist

<p>Run this before every payroll cycle:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Start from <strong>available</strong> cash (balance minus pending payments)</li>
  <li>Map the next <strong>two</strong> payroll dates and their fully loaded amounts</li>
  <li>Add only inflows you're confident clear in time, dated by <em>actual</em> payment behavior</li>
  <li>Subtract every committed outflow due before each payroll date</li>
  <li>Check the projected balance on each date against your minimum buffer</li>
  <li>If any date lands below the buffer, identify the specific inflow to chase or outflow to defer now, while you have time</li>
  <li>Re-run it whenever a big invoice, contract, or expense changes</li>
</ul>
