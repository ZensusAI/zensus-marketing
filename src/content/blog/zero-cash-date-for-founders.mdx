## What Is a Zero Cash Date?

<p><strong>A zero cash date is the day your projected cash balance reaches zero.</strong></p>

<p>It answers a simple question:</p>

<blockquote className="my-6 border-l-4 border-primary/30 pl-4 not-italic text-muted-foreground">
  <p>On what date will we run out of money if nothing changes?</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Unlike runway, which is often expressed in months, a <strong>zero cash date</strong> gives a specific point in time.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Examples:</p>

<ul>
  <li>September 12</li>
  <li>November 3</li>
  <li>January 18</li>
</ul>

<p>This makes it easier to plan fundraising timelines, hiring decisions, vendor commitments, payroll obligations, and cost reductions.</p>

## What Is the Difference Between Runway and Zero Cash Date?

<p><strong>Runway estimates how long cash will last. Zero cash date identifies exactly when cash reaches zero.</strong></p>

<p>Runway is typically calculated as:</p>

<p><strong>Runway = Cash on Hand ÷ Monthly Net Burn</strong></p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Example:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Cash balance = $300,000</li>
  <li>Monthly burn = $50,000</li>
  <li>Runway = 6 months</li>
</ul>

<BlogFigure
  src="/blog/zero-cash-date-for-founders/runway-vs-zero-cash-date.png"
  alt="Runway versus zero cash date comparison: $300K cash divided by $50K monthly burn suggests six months, but weekly cash balance hits zero at month 4.5"
  caption="Runway is an estimate. Zero cash date is a deadline. Cash timing matters more than monthly averages."
/>

<p>This formula assumes cash leaves the business evenly throughout each month. In reality, cash rarely behaves that way.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Most startups experience:</p>

<ul>
  <li>payroll every two weeks or monthly</li>
  <li>large software renewals</li>
  <li>vendor invoices</li>
  <li>tax payments</li>
  <li>delayed customer collections</li>
</ul>

<p>The timing of those events matters. That is why sophisticated finance teams track both runway and zero cash date. For the full vocabulary on burn rate and runway, see <a href="/blog/runway-vs-burn-rate-for-founders" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">runway vs burn rate</a>.</p>

## Why Can Runway Be Wrong?

<p><strong>Monthly runway calculations ignore cash timing.</strong></p>

<p>A company can have six months of calculated runway and a much earlier <strong>cash out date</strong> because expenses and collections rarely occur evenly.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Imagine:</p>

<ul>
  <li>payroll occurs on the 1st and 15th</li>
  <li>customers pay late</li>
  <li>annual software contracts renew unexpectedly</li>
  <li>a large vendor invoice arrives</li>
</ul>

<p>The average monthly burn may remain unchanged. The actual cash position may not. This creates what finance teams call <strong>timing risk</strong>.</p>

## How Do You Calculate a Zero Cash Date?

<p><strong>A zero cash date is calculated by forecasting cash inflows and outflows over time until the projected balance reaches zero.</strong></p>

<BlogFigure
  src="/blog/zero-cash-date-for-founders/how-to-calculate-zero-cash-date.png"
  alt="Five-step zero cash date formula: opening cash plus cash in minus cash out equals weekly balance, then identify the zero cash date"
  caption="Forecast cash by week, not by month: opening cash, plus inflows, minus outflows, until the balance reaches zero."
/>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">The process:</p>

### Step 1: Start With Current Cash

<p>Example: <strong>current cash balance = $300,000</strong></p>

### Step 2: Forecast Expected Cash Inflows

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Examples:</p>

<ul>
  <li>customer collections</li>
  <li>subscription revenue</li>
  <li>contract payments</li>
  <li>financing proceeds</li>
</ul>

### Step 3: Forecast Expected Cash Outflows

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Examples:</p>

<ul>
  <li>payroll</li>
  <li>rent</li>
  <li>software</li>
  <li>contractors</li>
  <li>taxes</li>
  <li>loan payments</li>
</ul>

### Step 4: Forecast by Week

<p>Instead of monthly averages, model each week separately. This is the core of <strong>how to calculate zero cash date</strong> accurately.</p>

### Step 5: Identify When Cash Reaches Zero

<p>The week where projected cash becomes negative contains your zero cash date. That date becomes your operational deadline.</p>

## Why Should Startups Forecast Weekly Instead of Monthly?

<p><strong>Weekly forecasting captures cash timing. Monthly forecasting hides it.</strong></p>

<p>Founders often ask how to calculate zero cash date accurately. The answer is almost always: use <strong>weekly cash flow forecasting</strong>, not monthly averages alone.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Weekly forecasting reveals:</p>

<ul>
  <li>payroll concentration</li>
  <li>collection delays</li>
  <li>vendor payment spikes</li>
  <li>seasonal cash swings</li>
</ul>

<p>Monthly averages smooth these events away. Unfortunately, your bank account does not operate on averages. For the broader framework, see <a href="/blog/what-is-cash-flow-forecasting" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">what is cash flow forecasting</a>.</p>

## Why Does Weekly Cash Timing Matter So Much?

<p>Most founders discover timing problems only after cash becomes tight.</p>

### Payroll Clustering

<p>Many startups process payroll twice monthly. Cash outflows are concentrated rather than evenly distributed. Map pay-cycle risk with the <a href="/tools/payroll-calendar" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">payroll calendar calculator</a>.</p>

### Delayed Customer Payments

<p>Invoices are not cash. Customers often pay 15, 30, or 60 days late. Collections timing directly impacts your zero cash date. See <a href="/blog/arr-vs-cash-for-founders" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">ARR vs cash</a> for why revenue and bank balance diverge.</p>

### Vendor Payment Spikes

<p>Annual contracts often create sudden cash outflows: AWS commitments, software renewals, insurance, security tooling.</p>

### Tax Payments

<p>Taxes rarely occur evenly throughout the year. Quarterly obligations can move a cash out date forward significantly.</p>
