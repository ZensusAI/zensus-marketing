## What Is the Difference Between Runway and Burn Rate?


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">The simplest explanation is:</p>


<blockquote className="my-6 border-l-4 border-primary/30 pl-4 not-italic text-muted-foreground">

  <p className="mb-2"><strong className="text-foreground">Burn rate is how fast you spend money.</strong></p>

  <p><strong className="text-foreground">Runway is how long you can survive at that spending rate.</strong></p>

</blockquote>


<p>Many founders use the terms interchangeably, but they measure completely different things.</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Imagine a startup with:</p>


<ul>

  <li>$1.2 million in the bank</li>
  <li>$100,000 monthly net burn</li>

</ul>


<p>The burn rate is <strong>$100,000 per month</strong>.</p>


<p>The runway is <strong>12 months</strong>.</p>


<p>One measures speed. The other measures time.</p>

## What Is Burn Rate?


<p>Burn rate measures how much money a startup spends over a specific period.</p>


<p>Most founders discuss burn rate monthly.</p>


<p>However, there are actually multiple types of burn. This is where confusion begins.</p>


### Gross Burn Rate


<p>Gross burn measures total monthly cash outflows.</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Example:</p>


<ul>

  <li><strong>Payroll:</strong> $80,000</li>
  <li><strong>Marketing:</strong> $10,000</li>
  <li><strong>Software:</strong> $5,000</li>
  <li><strong>Rent:</strong> $5,000</li>

</ul>


<p><strong>Gross burn = $100,000/month</strong></p>


<p>Gross burn tells investors how much cash leaves the business. It does not account for revenue.</p>


### Net Burn Rate


<p>Net burn is the metric most investors care about.</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Formula:</p>


<p><code className="rounded bg-muted px-1.5 py-0.5 text-sm">Net Burn = Cash Outflows − Cash Inflows</code></p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Example:</p>


<ul>

  <li>Monthly expenses: $100,000</li>
  <li>Monthly revenue: $40,000</li>

</ul>


<p><strong>Net burn = $60,000/month</strong></p>


<p>This represents the amount of cash the company actually loses each month.</p>


<p>When founders ask how to calculate startup burn rate, they are usually referring to net burn.</p>


### Cash Burn vs Net Burn


<p>Many founders use cash burn and net burn interchangeably. That is usually acceptable in casual conversation.</p>


<p>But during fundraising discussions, precision matters. Investors generally want to know:</p>


<ul>

  <li>gross burn</li>
  <li>net burn</li>
  <li>cash balance</li>
  <li>runway</li>

</ul>


<p>Each metric tells a different story.</p>

## How Do You Calculate Startup Runway?


<p>The classic startup runway calculation is simple.</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Runway formula:</p>


<p><code className="rounded bg-muted px-1.5 py-0.5 text-sm">Runway = Cash on Hand ÷ Monthly Net Burn</code></p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Example:</p>


<ul>

  <li>Cash balance: $2,000,000</li>
  <li>Net burn: $125,000/month</li>

</ul>


<p><strong>Runway = 16 months</strong></p>


<p>This is the startup runway formula most founders learn first.</p>


<p>However, there is a problem. The formula assumes:</p>


<ul>

  <li>revenue remains constant</li>
  <li>expenses remain constant</li>
  <li>hiring plans do not change</li>
  <li>customer churn remains stable</li>

</ul>


<p>Real businesses rarely behave that way.</p>


## Why Spreadsheet Runway Calculations Are Usually Wrong


<p>A startup may calculate 18 months of runway on the first day of the month.</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Five days later:</p>


<ul>

  <li>a new hire joins</li>
  <li>a major customer churns</li>
  <li>infrastructure costs increase</li>
  <li><a href="/blog/forecasting-ai-compute-costs-for-founders" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">AI compute expenses</a> spike</li>

</ul>


<p>The runway changes immediately. Static runway calculations become inaccurate surprisingly quickly.</p>


<p>This is why modern finance teams increasingly use <a href="/blog/what-is-a-13-week-cash-flow-forecast" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">rolling forecasts</a> rather than static spreadsheets.</p>


<p>Instead of relying on cash balance divided by burn, they continuously update assumptions using:</p>


<ul>

  <li>bank balances</li>
  <li>accounting data</li>
  <li>subscription revenue</li>
  <li>payroll expenses</li>
  <li>sales pipeline</li>

</ul>


<p>This produces a much more accurate runway forecast.</p>

## What Is a Burn Multiple?


<p>If runway and burn rate were the most important startup metrics in 2021, burn multiple has become the metric investors increasingly watch in 2026.</p>


<p>Burn multiple was popularized by David Sacks as a way to measure capital efficiency.</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Burn multiple formula:</p>


<p><code className="rounded bg-muted px-1.5 py-0.5 text-sm">Burn Multiple = Net Burn ÷ Net New ARR</code></p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Example:</p>


<ul>

  <li>Net burn: $200,000/month</li>
  <li>Net new ARR created: $100,000</li>

</ul>


<p><strong>Burn multiple = 2x</strong></p>


<BlogFigure
  src="/blog/runway-vs-burn-rate-for-founders/burn-multiple-formula.png"
  alt="Burn multiple formula comparing an efficient startup with high ARR growth per dollar burned versus an inefficient startup with low growth relative to cash burn"
  caption="Burn multiple measures capital efficiency: how much net new ARR each dollar of burn produces. Investors reward lower multiples."
/>


<p>In plain English: for every dollar of new recurring revenue generated, the company spent two dollars.</p>

## What Is a Good Burn Multiple in 2026?


<p>Investor expectations have changed dramatically.</p>


<ul>

  <li><strong>Burn multiple under 1x:</strong> exceptional</li>
  <li><strong>Burn multiple between 1x and 2x:</strong> strong</li>
  <li><strong>Burn multiple above 2x:</strong> potential concern</li>

</ul>


<p>Many investors now treat a <a href="https://sacks.substack.com/p/the-burn-multiple-51a7e43cb200" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">burn multiple below 2x</a> as the minimum threshold for a capital-efficient startup.</p>


<p>This has become one of the most important startup fundraising metrics in 2026.</p>


## How Much Runway Should a Startup Have in 2026?


<p>One of the most common questions founders ask today is how much runway investors expect in 2026.</p>


<p>The benchmark has shifted significantly.</p>


<ul>

  <li><strong>In 2021:</strong> typical runway target of 12–15 months</li>
  <li><strong>In 2026:</strong> typical runway target of 18–24 months</li>

</ul>


<p>Fundraising cycles are longer. Capital is more selective. Companies need more time to reach meaningful milestones before raising again.</p>


<p>Most investors now expect startups to maintain at least 18 months of runway after a funding round closes.</p>

<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Typical burn by stage:</p>


<ul>

  <li><strong>Pre-seed:</strong> ~$100K per quarter</li>

<p>These numbers vary by geography and industry, but they provide useful reference points for founders evaluating their own burn rate.</p>
