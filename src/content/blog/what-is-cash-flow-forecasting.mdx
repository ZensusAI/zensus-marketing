## What Is Cash Flow Forecasting?


<p>Cash flow forecasting is the process of estimating future cash inflows and outflows over a specific period of time.</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">A cash flow forecast helps businesses understand:</p>


<ul>

  <li>how much cash will be available</li>
  <li>when expenses are due</li>
  <li>whether liquidity levels are healthy</li>
  <li>how future financial obligations can be managed</li>

</ul>


<p>Forecasting gives finance leaders the visibility needed to make informed business decisions and maintain operational stability.</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Typical forecast inputs include:</p>


<ul>

  <li>customer payments</li>
  <li>payroll expenses</li>
  <li>vendor payments</li>
  <li>taxes</li>
  <li>debt repayments</li>
  <li>operational expenses</li>
  <li>capital expenditures</li>

</ul>


<p>The primary objective is to predict future cash movement as accurately as possible.</p>

## Types of Cash Flow Forecasting


<p>Different forecasting methods serve different business objectives.</p>


## Direct Cash Flow Forecasting


<p>The direct method forecasts actual expected cash receipts and payments.</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">This includes:</p>


<ul>

  <li>customer collections</li>
  <li>payroll expenses</li>
  <li>supplier payments</li>
  <li>rent and operational expenses</li>

</ul>


<p>Direct forecasting provides detailed short term visibility and is commonly used for treasury operations and liquidity management.</p>


## Indirect Cash Flow Forecasting


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">The indirect method starts with projected financial statements and adjusts for:</p>


<ul>

  <li>depreciation</li>
  <li>working capital changes</li>
  <li>non cash expenses</li>

</ul>


<p>This method is often used for:</p>


<p>FP&A processes</p>


<ul>

  <li>strategic planning</li>
  <li>long term financial forecasting</li>

</ul>


## What Is a Rolling Cash Flow Forecast?


<p>A rolling cash flow forecast continuously updates as time progresses.</p>


<p>Instead of creating a fixed annual forecast, businesses continuously extend the forecasting window.</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">For example:</p>


<ul>

  <li>a 13 week cash flow forecast may update weekly</li>
  <li>once one week is completed, another week is added</li>

</ul>


<p>This allows finance teams to maintain continuous visibility into future liquidity. For example, a startup planning new hires may use a rolling forecast to determine whether projected cash reserves can support additional operational expenses over the next quarter.</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Rolling forecasts have become increasingly popular because they improve:</p>


<ul>

  <li>forecast accuracy</li>
  <li>business agility</li>
  <li>responsiveness to changing conditions</li>

</ul>
