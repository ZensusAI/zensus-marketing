## What Does Default Alive Mean?


<p>The original <strong>default alive</strong> definition remains unchanged:</p>


<p><strong>A startup is default alive if it can reach profitability before running out of money without raising additional capital.</strong></p>


<p>Importantly: default alive does <strong>not</strong> mean profitable today.</p>


<p>A company can still be losing money and be default alive. What matters is the trajectory.</p>


<p>If current growth, margins, and spending trends naturally lead to profitability before cash reaches zero, the company is default alive.</p>


<p>This is one reason founders often misunderstand the concept. They assume default alive means profitable. It doesn't. It means survival is possible without another fundraising event.</p>


<p>Paul Graham introduced the framework in his essay <a href="http://paulgraham.com/aord.html" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">Default Alive or Default Dead</a>. <strong>Who created the default alive framework?</strong> Paul Graham, in October 2015. <strong>Why is default alive important for startups?</strong> Because it answers whether the business can reach sustainability on its own, which is the core question behind startup survival metrics and fundraising readiness.</p>


### Am I Default Alive?


<p><strong>How to know if my startup is default alive:</strong> if you never raised another dollar, would current revenue growth and margin improvements eventually make the business profitable before your cash balance reaches zero?</p>


<p>If yes, you're likely default alive.</p>


<p>If no, you're likely default dead.</p>


## What Does Default Dead Mean?


<p><strong>What does default dead mean?</strong> A startup is default dead if current cash plus expected growth is insufficient to reach profitability before running out of money.</p>


<p>This does not necessarily mean the company is failing. Many successful startups have spent periods as default dead.</p>


<p>The distinction is that default dead companies require an external event to survive. Usually one of:</p>


<ul>

  <li>a funding round</li>
  <li>venture debt</li>
  <li>a bridge round</li>
  <li>a major revenue acceleration</li>

</ul>


<p>Without one of those events, the company eventually runs out of cash.</p>


### Can a Default Dead Startup Still Raise Money?


<p><strong>Can a default dead startup raise money?</strong> Absolutely. Many venture-backed startups are default dead.</p>


<p>The key difference is whether investors can see a credible path to becoming default alive.</p>


## What Is the Difference Between Default Alive and Default Dead?


<p><strong>What is the difference between default alive and default dead?</strong> Default alive means profitability arrives before cash runs out on the current path. Default dead means it doesn't, unless something external changes the trajectory.</p>


<p>One measures sustainability. The other measures dependency on the next capital event.</p>

## The Four Numbers That Determine Default Alive in 2026


<p>Paul Graham's original framework can now be translated into four practical metrics. Together they form a practical <strong>startup capital efficiency checklist</strong> and a workable <strong>default alive calculation</strong>.</p>

### 1. Burn Multiple


<p><strong>Startup burn multiple</strong> has become one of the strongest indicators of startup capital efficiency.</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Formula:</p>


<p><code className="rounded bg-muted px-1.5 py-0.5 text-sm">Burn Multiple = Net Burn ÷ Net New ARR</code></p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Example:</p>


<ul>

  <li>Net burn: $200,000/month</li>
  <li>Net new ARR: $120,000/month</li>

</ul>


<p><strong>Burn multiple = 1.67x</strong></p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">What is a good burn multiple in 2026?</p>


<ul>

  <li><strong>Under 1x:</strong> exceptional</li>
  <li><strong>Between 1x and 2x:</strong> strong</li>
  <li><strong>Above 2x:</strong> potential concern</li>

</ul>


<p>For many software startups, a burn multiple below 2x has become the modern <strong>startup fundability benchmarks 2026</strong> threshold. For the full breakdown, see <a href="/blog/runway-vs-burn-rate-for-founders" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">runway vs burn rate</a>.</p>


### 2. Runway


<p>The second factor is <strong>startup runway</strong>.</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Formula:</p>


<p><code className="rounded bg-muted px-1.5 py-0.5 text-sm">Runway = Cash on Hand ÷ Monthly Net Burn</code></p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Example:</p>


<ul>

  <li>Cash balance: $1.8M</li>
  <li>Net burn: $100K/month</li>

</ul>


<p><strong>Runway = 18 months</strong></p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">How much runway should a startup have in 2026?</p>


<ul>

  <li><strong>In 2021:</strong> typical target of 12 to 15 months</li>
  <li><strong>In 2026:</strong> typical <strong>startup runway benchmark</strong> of 18 to 24 months</li>

</ul>


<p>Fundraising takes longer. Markets are more selective. Companies need more time to hit milestones.</p>


<p><strong>Is 12 months of runway enough?</strong> Increasingly, no. Most investors prefer to see at least 18 months of runway after a financing event.</p>

### 3. Gross Margin


<p>The third factor is <strong>startup gross margin</strong> and overall <strong>startup financial health</strong>.</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Formula:</p>


<p><code className="rounded bg-muted px-1.5 py-0.5 text-sm">Gross Margin = Gross Profit ÷ Revenue</code></p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Software companies:</p>


<p>Healthy benchmark: <strong>70%+ gross margin</strong></p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">AI-heavy companies:</p>


<p>Healthy benchmark: <strong>50% to 60% gross margin</strong></p>


<p>The difference exists because AI businesses often have meaningful inference and infrastructure costs. See <a href="/blog/forecasting-ai-compute-costs-for-founders" className="font-medium text-primary underline-offset-4 hover:underline">forecasting AI compute costs</a> for how variable compute spend affects margins and <strong>startup unit economics</strong>.</p>


<p><strong>Can an AI startup be default alive with lower margins?</strong> Yes. Investors understand AI companies operate differently. However, lower margins mean the company must compensate through stronger growth or lower burn.</p>


### 4. Growth Rate vs Burn Growth


<p>The final factor is often ignored in a <strong>startup burn rate</strong> snapshot alone.</p>


<p>Revenue growth must outpace burn growth.</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Company A:</p>


<ul>

  <li>Revenue growing 12% monthly</li>
  <li>Burn growing 3% monthly</li>

</ul>


<p>Likely moving toward default alive.</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Company B:</p>


<ul>

  <li>Revenue growing 6% monthly</li>
  <li>Burn growing 10% monthly</li>

</ul>


<p>Likely moving toward default dead.</p>


<p>Growth alone is not enough. What matters is whether the business is becoming more sustainable over time.</p>


<p><strong>Can a startup be growing and still be default dead?</strong> Absolutely. Many startups grow revenue while increasing expenses even faster. That creates growth without sustainability.</p>


## The Quick Default Alive Calculation


<p>Most founders don't need a complex model to run a first-pass <strong>default alive calculation</strong>. Start with these three metrics:</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Runway:</p>


<p><code className="rounded bg-muted px-1.5 py-0.5 text-sm">Cash on Hand ÷ Net Monthly Burn</code></p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Burn multiple:</p>


<p><code className="rounded bg-muted px-1.5 py-0.5 text-sm">Net Burn ÷ Net New ARR</code></p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Gross margin:</p>


<p><code className="rounded bg-muted px-1.5 py-0.5 text-sm">Gross Profit ÷ Revenue</code></p>


<p>Then ask:</p>


<blockquote className="my-6 border-l-4 border-primary/30 pl-4 not-italic text-muted-foreground">

  <p>If these trends continue, do we become profitable before runway reaches zero?</p>

</blockquote>


<p>That is essentially the default alive calculation. ## What To Do If You Are Default Dead


<p>Discovering you're default dead isn't necessarily bad news. The important thing is acting early. This is the practical side of <strong>how to become default alive</strong>.</p>

### Reduce Burn


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Focus on:</p>


<ul>

  <li>delaying non-essential hires</li>
  <li>reducing contractor spend</li>
  <li>renegotiating vendor contracts</li>
  <li>eliminating low-ROI expenses</li>

</ul>


<p>The goal is not cutting costs blindly. The goal is improving capital efficiency.</p>


### Accelerate Revenue


<p>Many founders immediately focus on reducing burn. Often the better answer is increasing revenue.</p>


<p className="mb-2 font-medium text-foreground">Examples:</p>


<ul>

  <li>improve conversion rates</li>
  <li>shorten sales cycles</li>
  <li>increase pricing</li>
  <li>improve expansion revenue</li>
  <li>launch high-impact features</li>

</ul>


<p>Revenue growth can move a company toward default alive faster than cost cutting alone.</p>


### Explore Transitional Capital


<p>Sometimes companies need more time. Options include bridge rounds, SAFE extensions, and venture debt.</p>


<p>The objective is not permanent dependency on fundraising. The objective is creating enough runway to become sustainably default alive.</p>
